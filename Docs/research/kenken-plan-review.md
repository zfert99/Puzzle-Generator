# Engineering Plan Review: KenKen/Calcudoku Implementation Plan

## TL;DR

- **This is a strong, well-scoped, technically sound plan.** Its core domain claims about KenKen/Calcudoku are accurate, its slice ordering (K0→K5) is logically gated, and it correctly identifies the single most dangerous domain-specific pitfall (multiset vs. set cage tables). It is ready to start, with a handful of corrections and additions noted below.
- **Two factual claims need softening from "convention" to "common design choice":** the 2-cell restriction on subtraction/division cages is a *Will Shortz/NYT and common-implementation* convention, not a universal rule (calcudoku.org and others explicitly allow larger −/÷ cages), and the "÷ < − < × < +" operator-difficulty ordering is a reasonable heuristic but not a rigorously validated total order.
- **The plan's cage-generator fix ("terminate on target size alone") is under-specified versus the reference generators it cites.** Both Simon Tatham's `keen.c` and KDE KSudoku use *additional* mechanisms (a hard size cap, cage-quality/ambiguity rejection, single-cell-cage proportion bands, uniqueness-driven regeneration). The plan should adopt these rather than rely on target size alone.

---

## Key Findings

1. **Rules baseline: fully correct.** N×N Latin square, no boxes, cages with target + operator, and — critically — digits *may* repeat in a cage as long as they don't share a row/column. Wikipedia states it verbatim: "Digits may be repeated within a cage, as long as they are not in the same row or column," and explicitly, "(Unlike Killer Sudoku, digits may repeat within a cage.)" This is the defining distinction from Killer Sudoku and is accurately stated.
2. **Grid sizes: correct, including prime sizes.** 4×4/6×6 are the NYT/Shortz daily standard; 5×5 and 7×7 are genuinely common in published Calcudoku. Grids range 3×3–9×9 across the ecosystem (Wikipedia). The Latin-square-only structural argument for allowing any N≥3 is sound.
3. **Operator difficulty ("÷ < − < × < +"): directionally correct as a heuristic, not a validated total order.** The underlying claim — 2-cell ÷/− cages are tightly constrained while large + cages are ambiguous, and × is more constrained than + — is well supported. The strict total ordering is an oversimplification.
4. **2-cell −/÷ restriction: a strong convention, not universal.** Correct for NYT/Shortz, KSudoku, and Tatham's Keen, but explicitly *not* universal (calcudoku.org allows any cage size with a largest-first rule). The plan's "by construction" enforcement is a safe, defensible choice.
5. **Trademark: correct, with a registrant/date nuance.** "KenKen" is a registered trademark; "Calcudoku"/"Mathdoku" are the established generic alternatives. Per USPTO/Justia the mark is now held by **KenKen Puzzle LLC** (originally registered by **Nextoy, LLC**), first filed **June 1, 2007** — not the inventor personally.
6. **Solving techniques: correct, including the arithmetic.** N(N+1)/2 = 21 for N=6 and 6! = 720 are correct; "Rule of 21"/"Rule of 720," prime factorization for ×, and cage-combination restriction are all real, documented human techniques.
7. **Cage-generator termination concern: valid, but the proposed fix is thin.** Reference generators use more than target size.
8. **Multiset cage tables: correctly identified as the #1 pitfall.** This is accurate and is the strongest single insight in the plan.
9. **Boxless-grid K0 slice: sound software engineering.** Isolating a closed-union-type widening before dependent features is a well-established refactoring pattern.
10. **Difficulty grading: consistent with best practice.** Technique-tier + frequency scoring, calibrated per size, matches how Sudoku-family puzzles (and KenKen specifically) are graded. The weaker-propagation caveat is real and correctly flagged.
11. **Bitmask/MRV backtracking: appropriate, with the right caveat.** Fine for KenKen, but weaker Latin-square propagation makes the cage-multiset pruning (intersected with row/col masks) essential rather than optional.
12. **Overall: a high-quality, appropriately scoped plan** for a solo/small-team developer, with a comprehensive risk table and only minor gaps.

## Details

### (a) Rule / convention accuracy checks

**Rules baseline (Claim 1) — CONFIRMED.** Every reliable source agrees with the plan. KenKen/Calcudoku is an N×N Latin square (each digit 1..N once per row and column), **with no sub-boxes** — the box constraint of Sudoku is *replaced* by arithmetic cages. Digits **may repeat within a cage** provided the repeated digits do not share a row or column. Wikipedia, the KDE KSudoku handbook, university math-circle materials (UNC Charlotte, Berkeley BMC), and numerous publishers all state this explicitly (Wikipedia: "Digits may be repeated within a cage, as long as they are not in the same row or column"). The plan's framing — that this duplicate-in-cage rule "changes the math" and is the reason cage-combination tables must be multisets — is exactly right and is the single most important correctness insight in the document.

**Grid sizes (Claim 2) — CONFIRMED, including primes.** The NYT/Will Shortz KenKen product ships **4×4 and 6×6** as its standard daily/booklet sizes; the St. Martin's Griffin edition (ISBN 9780312603212) states verbatim: "This New York Times edition of KenKen contains 300 easy to hard 4x4 and 6x6 size puzzles, with 'How to Solve' instructions and an introduction by puzzlemaster Will Shortz." Grids range 3×3–9×9 across the ecosystem (Wikipedia). The plan's structural argument is correct: because KenKen is Latin-square-only, **any N≥3 is valid**, including primes 5 and 7 — unlike box-Sudoku variants, which need N with clean box factorizations (4, 6, 9, …). Prime-size grids are genuinely common in published Calcudoku: multiple commercial puzzle books ship 5×5 and 7×7 sets (e.g., the "240 Mixed Grid 4×4, 5×5, 6×6, 7×7" and Djape's "Massive Book of CalcuDoku" 4×4–8×8 collections), and online generators offer 3×3–9×9 including 5×5 and 7×7. The proposed ladder (4/6 first, add 5/7 next, hold 9) is well-justified and matches the "mini infrastructure" mirroring rationale. **One nuance:** the plan says "NYT ships 4×4/6×6 daily." NYT/Shortz's *canonical product* is 4×4 and 6×6; the plan should not over-claim that primes are a *NYT* convention — they are a *broader-ecosystem* convention (KrazyDad "Inkies," calcudoku.org, puzzle books, Tatham's Keen which generates any size 3–9). This is a minor framing point, not an error.

**Operator difficulty ordering (Claim 3) — DIRECTIONALLY SOUND, but overstated as a strict total order.** The plan's rationale is well supported by the literature: two-cell ÷ and − cages have very small candidate sets (a "3÷" on a 6×6 is just {1,3},{2,6}; a "2−" is a handful of consecutive-difference pairs), while large + cages are the most ambiguous. Multiplication is widely described as *easier than it looks* ("The multiplication secret: Multiplication cages are often easier than they look because they have fewer valid combinations than addition cages" — Puzzolve) because factorization sharply limits combinations, whereas addition cages have the most partitions. So the *endpoints* of the claim (÷/− tightest, + loosest) are solidly grounded. **However**, the strict "÷ < − < × < +" total ordering is a simplification: candidate count depends jointly on **operator, target value, cage size, and N**, not operator alone. A "1−" cage on a 6×6 has five candidate pairs, while a "5−" has exactly one — so − is not uniformly easier than ×. The right lens is candidate/combination count *per specific (op, target, size, N)* — which is exactly what the plan's K1 multiset tables compute — rather than a fixed operator ranking. Recommendation: treat the ordering as a *default prior* for operator-mix difficulty tuning, but let measured combination counts drive actual grading. The plan mostly does this already (it makes multiset-combo ambiguity the "difficulty currency"), so this is a framing correction, not a design flaw.

**2-cell subtraction/division convention (Claim 4) — MOSTLY TRUE; correctly overstated-then-safely-enforced.** The 2-cell restriction on − and ÷ is real and widely followed, and the reason is non-associativity: 6−(4−1)=3 but (6−4)−1=1, so multi-cell −/÷ is ambiguous unless a "largest-first" convention is imposed. Wikipedia is explicit: "In the English-language KenKen books of Will Shortz, the issue of the non-associativity of division and subtraction is addressed by restricting clues based on either of those operations to cages of only two cells… Some puzzle authors have not done this and have published puzzles that use more than two cells for these operations." KDE KSudoku enforces it in code ("Division and subtraction operators… can only appear in cages of size 2"). Tatham's Keen enforces it (SUB/DIV only on size-2 blocks). **But it is not universal**: calcudoku.org and its "Advanced" puzzle books explicitly allow any cage size for −/÷, resolving order by largest-first ("The puzzles in this book (and indeed on calcudoku.org) allow any cage size"). So the plan's phrasing "NYT/Shortz convention" is accurate as attribution, and enforcing "−/÷ = size 2 by construction" is the safe, mainstream choice that avoids the non-associativity trap entirely. **Verdict: correct and well-handled**; the plan should just internally note this is a *design choice consistent with NYT*, not an inviolable rule of the puzzle type.

**Trademark (Claim 5) — CONFIRMED, with a registrant/date nuance.** "KenKen" is a registered trademark. The plan attributes it to "the inventor's company"; the precise chain is: invented by **Tetsuya Miyamoto** (2004); toy inventor **Robert Fuhrer** (owner of **Nextoy, LLC**) encountered the Japanese books in **2007** and brought the puzzle to the U.S. market in 2008; the "KenKen" mark was **first filed June 1, 2007** (serial 77195266) and the registration (Reg. #5146563, serial 87107903) is now held by **KenKen Puzzle LLC** (Justia lists Nextoy, LLC as "Original Registrant" and KenKen Puzzle LLC as "1st New Owner Entered After Registration"). "KenDoku" is also trademarked. Wikipedia states plainly: "The names Calcudoku and Mathdoku are sometimes used by those who do not have the rights to use the KenKen or KenDoku trademarks." This directly validates the plan's strategy: ship under a neutral/generic name ("MathCage," "Calcudoku," or "Mathdoku") and use "KenKen" only descriptively/nominatively. **This is sound risk avoidance** (general awareness, not legal advice). Minor correction for the plan's docs: attribute the mark to Nextoy / KenKen Puzzle LLC rather than to Miyamoto personally.

**Solving techniques and arithmetic (Claim 6) — CONFIRMED, arithmetic correct.** N(N+1)/2 for N=6 = 6·7/2 = **21** ✓, and 6! = 720 ✓. Both the "Rule of 21" (row/column sum) and "Rule of 720" (row/column product) are documented, named human techniques — Penny Dell Puzzles' official "How To Solve KenKen" PDF uses both names and even extends the product rule across multiple rows (720×720 = 518,400). Prime factorization for × cages, line/house invariants, naked pairs, X-wing on rows/columns, and cage-combination (cross-cage) restriction are all real techniques carried over from Sudoku or specific to KenKen. The plan's tier ladder (T1 singles + 2-cell ÷/− resolution; T2 pairs + × factorization; T3 line invariants + cage-combo restriction; T4 X-wing-class + multi-line invariants) is a sensible, literature-consistent progression. The billabob open-source KenKen solver independently grades on essentially the same technique ladder (naked/hidden singles, cage-region intersection, hidden pairs, X-Wing, Swordfish, cage-combination solver).

### (b) Algorithmic / technical soundness

**Cage-generation termination (Claim 7) — VALID CONCERN; PROPOSED FIX IS INADEQUATE AS STATED.** The plan's insight is correct: a Killer generator that grows a cage until adding another cell would force a duplicate ("boxes itself in") loses that natural stop in KenKen, where duplicates are legal. But "terminate on target size alone" is *weaker* than what the two reference implementations actually do — and the plan itself cites KSudoku as its shared engine, so it should match KSudoku's approach:

- **Simon Tatham's `keen.c`** does not use a per-cage random target at all. It (1) randomly places dominoes (probability 3/4), then (2) folds leftover singletons into neighbouring blocks, gated by a **hard global size cap `#define MAXBLK 6`** — with the verbatim comment "Maximum size of any clue block. Very large ones are annoying in UI terms… and also in solver terms (too many possibilities to iterate over)" — using a disjoint-set forest (DSF). If any singleton can't be placed, it **regenerates the whole grid** from a fresh Latin square. Operators are then assigned with quality filters (avoiding too-easy sums/differences), and a solver-driven loop **regenerates until the puzzle is both unique and exactly the requested difficulty** (with hard-coded difficulty exceptions to prevent the generator spinning forever).
- **KDE KSudoku's `CageGenerator`** grows a cage from a seed via `makeOneCage(seedCell, requiredSize)` — the comment says "a cage of a chosen size **(or less)**," i.e. it stops at `requiredSize` *or earlier* when neighbours run out. Crucially it layers on: a `maxSize` cap, a `maxValue` cap, a **`maxCombos` cap** (rejecting cages with too many valid digit-combinations — i.e. too-ambiguous cages), a `cageIsOK()` gate, and explicit **min/max single-cell-cage counts** (`mMinSingles`/`mMaxSingles`), all wrapped in a retry protocol driven by a DLX uniqueness check (return `0` = too many bad-cage failures → retry; `-1` = non-unique → retry).

So the established pattern is: **(1) a size cap, (2) cage-quality/ambiguity rejection, (3) uniqueness-driven regeneration, and (4) explicit single-cell-cage proportion control.** "Terminate on target size" covers only part of (1). **Recommendation:** the plan should (a) keep the hard `maxSize` cap, (b) add a single-cell-cage proportion band (too many single-cell "givens" makes trivial puzzles — a documented failure mode), and (c) rely on the K2 uniqueness verifier as the real backstop. This is a refinement, not a redesign — and it dovetails with the plan's own K4 note about re-evaluating `maxCombos`.

**Multiset cage-combination tables (Claim 8) — CORRECTLY IDENTIFIED AS THE #1 PITFALL.** This is the plan's strongest domain insight and it is accurate. Because digits may repeat within a KenKen cage (unlike Killer Sudoku), the enumeration of valid cage contents must be over **multisets** (e.g., a 3-cell "6×" on a 6×6 can be {1,1,6} or {1,2,3} — the {1,1,6} option is *only* legal if the two 1s land in different rows/columns), not distinct-digit sets. University materials give the canonical example: an L-shaped 3-cell "5+" cage yielding {1,3,1} or {2,1,2}-type multisets, feasible only under the row/column geometry. KSudoku encodes exactly this split: it has a Killer-only `hasDuplicates()` check *and* a separate `isSelfConsistent()` for Mathdoku (verbatim comment: "a Mathdoku cage can contain a digit more than once, but not in the same row/column"). The plan's mitigation — enforce repeat-legality at exactly **one** place (the solver's placement-time check) and fuzz 4×4 exhaustively — is the right architecture: it prevents the classic bug where a naive port reuses Killer's set-based tables and silently rejects valid solutions or accepts invalid ones. **One subtlety to add:** the multiset table gives *which multisets are arithmetically valid*, but geometric feasibility (can these repeats actually be placed given the cage's shape?) must be checked against cage geometry at solve/generate time — the table alone cannot encode it. The plan's "masks + placement-time repeat check" split appears to handle this, but it should be called out explicitly as a two-layer check (arithmetic multiset validity → geometric placement legality).

**Bitmask/MRV backtracking vs. DLX (Claim 11) — APPROPRIATE, with the caveat correctly load-bearing.** Bitmask + MRV backtracking is a fine and common choice for KenKen (Tatham's Keen uses a Latin-square solver with backtracking only at the hardest tier; many GitHub CSP solvers use plain backtracking + constraint propagation). Dropping the box-mask is correct (no boxes). The important caveat — which the plan raises itself as Risk #10 — is that Latin-square propagation is **inherently weaker** than Sudoku's: each cell sits in only **2** constraining units (row, column) instead of 3 (row, column, box). This means naked/hidden-single cascades fire less often, so the solver leans harder on **cage constraints** to make progress. The practical implication: the cage-multiset pruning must be **intersected with the row/col candidate masks** and pushed aggressively (the analogue of the "cage-sum-reachability" pruning identified for the Killer plan), or backtracking will balloon on 6×6+ QuadOp shapes. The plan's K2 design ("cage pruning via K1 masks plus placement-time repeat check") addresses this, but the review flags that this pruning is **essential, not optional**, for performance — and the K2 gate ("uniqueness verify <50ms avg at 6×6") is the right place to catch it if it's insufficient. Note that KSudoku itself uses **DLX** (Knuth's Dancing Links) for its uniqueness check; the plan's choice to use bitmask/MRV instead is justified by the user's prior Next.js/WASM deployment constraints, and is acceptable *provided* the cage pruning is strong. If 9×9 QuadOp is ever added, DLX (or a hybrid) may become worth reconsidering because the plan's own Risk #1 (multiset table explosion, targets up to 9! = 362,880 for ×) compounds with weaker propagation at that size.

**Difficulty grading (Claim 10) — CONSISTENT WITH BEST PRACTICE.** The reuse of a technique-tier + frequency ("raw × density") scorer, calibrated per grid size via re-measured bands, matches the mainstream approach: solvers grade by the **hardest technique required** (the Sudoku Explainer / SE model) optionally combined with **how often** hard techniques recur (the HoDoKu "total work" model) — the plan's "raw × density" two-factor score is essentially a blend of both, which is a defensible and common design. Per-size recalibration is necessary and correctly planned (4×4 has too few cells for the same bands as 6×6 — the plan flags band compression at 4×4 as expected). The KenKen-specific things a generic reused pipeline could miss, which the plan should watch: (1) the weaker 2-unit propagation (already Risk #10) means the *same* technique tier yields *fewer* forced moves, so bands must be re-fit, not merely rescaled; (2) operator-mix (SingleOp→DualOp→QuadOp) is an independent difficulty axis that interacts with size — the plan treats it as first-class, which is correct; (3) "No-Op/Mystery" mode (operator hidden) is a genuinely different difficulty regime requiring uniqueness across all operator interpretations — correctly **deferred**. The plan's instinct to make **multiset-combo ambiguity the difficulty currency** (and to re-test `maxCombos` as a lever, which KSudoku uses as its primary knob) is exactly aligned with how KSudoku controls Mathdoku difficulty.

### (c) Plan structure and sequencing quality

The **K0→K5 slicing is logically ordered and appropriately gated**, and the gate discipline (each slice has explicit exit criteria) is a strength carried over from the Killer plans.

- **K0 (boxless-grid foundation) as a prerequisite is correct engineering (Claim 9).** Widening a closed union type (`GridSize = 4|6|9`) and auditing every exhaustive consumer (config, API allowlists, UI pickers, on-screen renderer, PDF export) *before* building dependent features is a textbook "expand the closed type, then chase every non-exhaustive/assuming consumer" refactor. Box assumptions (thick box borders, box-peer highlighting, `boxWidth`/`boxHeight`) are meaningless for boxless 5×5/7×7 and would produce silent rendering artifacts if not gated on a `hasBoxes` flag first. Doing this as an isolated, regression-green slice (classic 4/6/9 + Killer 6/9 still pass) is exactly right and de-risks everything downstream. The `HumanSolver` size guard (throw on unsupported size rather than silently assuming 3×3 boxes) is a good defensive touch.
- **K1 (multiset tables + operator model) correctly precedes the solver (K2)** — the tables are the foundation the solver prunes against, and gating K1 on "spot-tests against published examples" is the right way to catch the multiset bug early.
- **K2→K3→K4→K5** (exact solver + generator → logical solver + tiers → difficulty configs + generation → surfaces/UI/dailies) mirrors the proven Killer sequence. The K5 "real 3-way discriminant" (replacing duck-typed `'cages'` detection with an explicit variant tag) is important and correctly placed — see Risk #7.

The plan's **"definition of done"** is concrete and testable (4×4 + 6×6 unique, banded, operator-aware, playable, printable, in daily registry, full battery green). Deferring 5/7 behind a follow-up slice while still landing K0 is a sensible scope-control decision.

### (d) Risks: well-covered vs. potentially missing

**Well-covered (the 11-item risk table is genuinely comprehensive):** multiset table explosion at 9×9 (targets to 9!), repeat-legality bugs, trademark, 4×4 band compression, cage-mate pencil-stripping regression (correctly *disabled* for KenKen since repeats are legal), per-variant size gating, duck-typed variant misclassification, boxless renderer phantom box lines, cage-generator termination, weaker Latin-square propagation, and save/resume round-tripping. This covers the domain, the algorithm, the UI, and persistence — a strong table.

**Potentially missing or under-weighted risks:**

1. **Single-cell-cage proportion control.** Neither the risk table nor K4 explicitly bounds the *number of single-cell "given" cages*. Too many single-cell cages produces trivially easy puzzles (a documented KenKen generation failure mode — the CanCan generator filters puzzles "with a proportion of single-cell cages above some maximum"; KSudoku has explicit `mMinSingles`/`mMaxSingles`). This should be an explicit generation parameter and a difficulty lever, not left implicit.
2. **Geometric feasibility of multiset repeats.** As noted, arithmetic multiset validity ≠ placeability. A cage shape that is a straight domino/line *cannot* hold repeats at all; an L/T shape can. The plan should explicitly verify that its "guaranteed/union mask" derivation accounts for cage *geometry*, not just arithmetic — otherwise it can over-count candidates for line-shaped cages and under-prune.
3. **Multiplication target overflow / table indexing at larger N.** The plan notes targets up to 9! for ×, but should confirm the memoization key (op, target, size, N) and integer types handle the largest products cleanly, and that lazy per-N table construction has a bounded worst-case build time (a perf, not correctness, risk — but worth a K1 budget note, which the plan gestures at).
4. **No-Op/Mystery uniqueness cost (when it's eventually built).** Correctly deferred, but worth flagging now that verifying uniqueness "across every operator interpretation" multiplies solver work — a future-scope cost the roadmap should carry.
5. **Difficulty non-monotonicity across sizes.** Because propagation strength changes with N, a "hard 5×5" may not sit cleanly between "hard 4×4" and "hard 6×6." The plan's per-size recalibration handles this, but the risk table doesn't explicitly name cross-size band *comparability* as something users may (wrongly) expect.

None of these are fatal; all are refinements to an already-thorough risk register.

### (e) Overall verdict

**This is a high-quality, appropriately-scoped, and technically sound implementation plan** — clearly written by someone who has internalized both the puzzle domain and their own codebase. It gets the hard domain distinctions right (Latin-square-only, duplicate-in-cage legality, multiset tables), sequences its work sensibly behind a well-justified foundational refactor (K0), reuses proven infrastructure judiciously, and carries a genuinely comprehensive risk table. The factual errors are minor and mostly matters of *attribution/over-claiming* (prime sizes as "NYT" convention; 2-cell −/÷ as universal rather than NYT-convention; operator difficulty as a strict order; trademark to the inventor rather than Nextoy/KenKen Puzzle LLC) rather than *substantive* errors. The one real technical gap is that the cage-generator's termination strategy is under-specified relative to the reference generators it cites (KSudoku, Tatham's Keen), which use size caps + ambiguity rejection + single-cell proportion control + uniqueness regeneration rather than target size alone.

**Readiness: GREEN — start, with the noted refinements folded into K1/K2/K4.**

## Recommendations

**Before starting (fold into planning docs):**

1. **Fix four attributions** in the plan text: (a) prime sizes are an *ecosystem-wide* convention, not NYT's (NYT = 4×4/6×6); (b) 2-cell −/÷ is a *Shortz/NYT convention and safe design choice*, not a universal rule; (c) operator difficulty "÷ < − < × < +" is a *default prior*, with actual grading driven by per-(op,target,size,N) combination counts; (d) attribute the KenKen trademark to Nextoy / KenKen Puzzle LLC (first filed 2007), not to Miyamoto personally.

**In K1 (multiset tables):**
2. Implement the two-layer check explicitly: **arithmetic multiset validity → geometric placement legality**. Verify guaranteed/union masks account for cage *shape* (line-shaped cages cannot hold repeats).
3. Confirm the memoization key and integer types handle × targets up to 9! (362,880) and document the lazy-build perf budget.

**In K2 (solver + generator):**
4. **Do not terminate cage growth on target size alone.** Adopt the reference-generator pattern: hard `maxSize` cap **+** cage-ambiguity rejection (`maxCombos`-style) **+** uniqueness-driven regeneration as the real backstop. Match KSudoku, since it is your cited shared engine, and note that Tatham's Keen uses a hard `MAXBLK = 6` cap plus whole-grid regeneration.
5. Treat **cage-multiset pruning intersected with row/col masks as mandatory**, not optional — it is load-bearing given 2-unit (weaker) propagation. Keep the <50ms 6×6 QuadOp gate as the tripwire.

**In K4 (difficulty + generation):**
6. Add an explicit **single-cell-cage proportion band** (min and max) as both a correctness guard (avoid trivial puzzles) and a difficulty lever.
7. Re-fit (don't merely rescale) difficulty bands per size, and set user-facing expectations that difficulty labels are **not** strictly comparable across grid sizes.

**Thresholds that would change these recommendations:**

- If **9×9 QuadOp** is promoted from "deferred" to in-scope: re-evaluate DLX (or a hybrid) versus bitmask/MRV, because multiset-table size (× targets to 362,880) compounds with weak propagation.
- If K2's uniqueness-verify **exceeds ~50ms avg at 6×6** or generation yield drops sharply: the cage pruning is insufficient — strengthen candidate intersection before adding search heuristics.
- If **generated puzzles feel trivial** despite passing band gates: the single-cell-cage proportion is almost certainly too high.

## Caveats

- **Convention vs. rule:** Several "conventions" in the plan (2-cell −/÷; operator difficulty order) are common design choices, not inviolable rules of the puzzle type. Sources genuinely disagree (calcudoku.org allows larger −/÷ cages). The plan's *enforcement* choices are safe and mainstream; only the *framing* needs softening.
- **KSudoku `.cpp` internals:** The cage-generator's growth loop was reconstructed from the KSudoku header comments and documented method contracts (`cagegenerator.h` — member variables `mMinSingles`/`mMaxSingles`/`mMaxCombos`, `makeOneCage(seedCell, requiredSize)` "or less", return-code retry protocol) plus verbatim `keen.c` code (`#define MAXBLK 6`, DSF domino/singleton folding); the KSudoku `cagegenerator.cpp` body itself was not retrievable in this review. The header evidence is strong, but line-level `.cpp` verification is recommended if you mirror its exact loop.
- **No legal advice:** The trademark discussion is for general awareness only. The generic-name strategy ("Calcudoku"/"Mathdoku"/neutral brand) is what other implementations use, but formal clearance is a matter for counsel if this becomes commercial.
- **Operator-difficulty literature is informal:** The "÷ < − < × < +" ordering reflects solver/community heuristics and puzzle-guide commentary, not a peer-reviewed ranking; empirical per-size measurement (which the plan builds) is the authoritative source.
